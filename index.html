Routine laboratory trajectories encode the onset of organ-level complications in cancer

A transformer trained on longitudinal laboratory data from 3,868 patients predicts 111 treatment-associated complications up to two years before clinical onset — using only routine blood work already collected in standard oncological care.

Abstract

Routine laboratory panels drawn during cancer treatment generate dense temporal records of organ function, yet this signal is discarded by prognostic tools that operate on single-timepoint snapshots. A transformer trained on laboratory trajectories from 3,868 patients with multiple myeloma or advanced ovarian cancer predicted the two-year onset of 111 treatment-associated complications, with 1.1- to 3.0-fold enrichment over prevalence across grouped endpoints. The temporal advantage over non-sequential baselines scaled with biological tempo, from two- to fivefold for gradually evolving endpoints such as myelodysplastic syndromes and type 2 diabetes to near parity for acute conditions. Predictions generalised across both cancers for shared toxicity endpoints but diverged for disease-specific complications, and biomarker masking recovered diagnosis-specific signatures consistent with established pathophysiology.

Key Findings
↗
Temporal signal scales with biological tempo
The transformer's advantage over cross-sectional baselines is largest for slowly evolving complications (2–5× for MDS, T2D, osteolyses) and smallest for acute conditions, demonstrating that trajectory information carries complication-specific signal inaccessible to single-timepoint analysis.
⟷
Biological coherence across cancers
Shared toxicity endpoints (cardiovascular, renal, infections, mortality) generalise across myeloma and ovarian cancer with near-identical performance. Disease-specific endpoints diverge along mechanistically expected lines.
◎
Interpretable biomarker signatures
Feature masking recovers diagnosis-specific patterns aligned with pathophysiology: eGFR for kidney disease, MCH/MCV for MDS, uric acid for diabetes, INR for infections — without prior biological constraints.
⬡
No additional testing required
All predictions derive from 39 routine laboratory analytes already collected at every clinical encounter. The data infrastructure for anticipatory toxicity management is already in place in standard oncological care.

Quick Start (available upon publication)
inference.py
from labtrajectory import HealthForecaster, LabSequence # Load pretrained model model = HealthForecaster.from_pretrained("labtrajectory/v1") # Create a laboratory sequence from routine blood work seq = LabSequence.from_dataframe( labs_df, analytes=model.analyte_list, # 39 routine analytes interval_days=(10, 30), normalize=True ) # Predict 2-year complication risks predictions = model.predict(seq) for dx, prob in predictions.top_k(5): print(f"{dx.name:<35} {prob:.3f}") # Cardiovascular diseases 0.412 # Kidney disease 0.287 # Bacterial infections 0.234 # Osteolyses 0.189 # Type 2 diabetes 0.143
