Routine laboratory trajectories encode the onset of organ-level complications in cancer

A transformer trained on longitudinal laboratory data from 3,868 patients predicts 111 treatment-associated complications up to two years before clinical onset — using only routine blood work already collected in standard oncological care.

Abstract

Routine laboratory panels drawn during cancer treatment generate dense temporal records of organ function, yet this signal is discarded by prognostic tools that operate on single-timepoint snapshots. A transformer trained on 2,777,595 laboratory measurements from 3,868 patients with multiple myeloma or ovarian cancer predicted two-year onset of 111 diagnoses spanning eight clinical categories, achieving 1.1- to 3.1-fold enrichment above prevalence at the group level. The temporal advantage over non-sequential baselines scaled with biological tempo, with AUROC gains of up to 0.08 for gradually evolving endpoints, narrowing for conditions with laboratory antecedents captured by single timepoints. Predictions generalised across both cancers for shared toxicity endpoints but diverged for disease-specific complications, and biomarker masking recovered diagnosis-specific signatures consistent with established pathophysiology. Cross-system external validation on an independent United States cohort confirmed transferability of mortality, metastatic disease, and myelodysplastic syndrome predictions, with remaining endpoints limited by structural coding incompatibilities between healthcare systems.

Key Findings
↗
Temporal signal scales with biological tempo
The transformer's advantage over cross-sectional baselines is largest for slowly evolving complications (ΔAUROC +0.08 for MDS, +0.07 for infections, +0.06 for kidney disease) and smallest for acute conditions, demonstrating that trajectory information carries complication-specific signal inaccessible to single-timepoint analysis.
⟷
Biological coherence across cancers
Six of eight grouped endpoints generalised across myeloma and ovarian cancer with AUROC differences ≤0.04. Disease-specific endpoints diverged along mechanistically expected lines: osteolyses confined to myeloma, MDS driven by platinum exposure in ovarian cancer.
◎
Interpretable biomarker signatures
Feature masking recovers diagnosis-specific patterns aligned with pathophysiology: eGFR for kidney disease, MCH/MCV for MDS, calcium and INR for diabetes, age and INR for infections — without prior biological constraints.
⬡
Cross-system external validation
Applied without retraining to 1,626 MIMIC-IV patients. Mortality, metastatic disease, and MDS predictions transferred across healthcare systems. All eight primary endpoints maintained enrichment at or above prevalence despite coding system differences.
⊕
No additional testing required
All predictions derive from 39 routine laboratory analytes already collected at every clinical encounter. The data infrastructure for anticipatory toxicity management is already in place in standard oncological care.
